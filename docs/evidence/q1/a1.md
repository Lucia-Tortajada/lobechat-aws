# Q1 — Use case design

## Context

A mid-sized Spanish NGO operating in the mental health sector employs approximately 80 staff members, among them clinical psychologists, social workers, and trained volunteers, distributed across 12 regional offices in cities including Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, and Seville. The organization provides free psychological support to vulnerable populations: young people at risk of social exclusion, victims of domestic violence, and individuals experiencing homelessness or addiction.

The organization handles an average of 300 to 500 cases per month. Each case generates session notes, referral documents, treatment protocols, and follow-up reports, all currently stored as unstructured Word documents and PDFs scattered across shared network drives and email threads. The administrative burden is substantial: clinicians dedicate around 35% of their time to documentation rather than direct patient care, with psychiatrists averaging approximately three hours per workday on documentation alone (Rasmussen et al., 2025), and behavioral health practitioners spend an average of 16 minutes per patient encounter on administrative tasks, with 50% reporting moderate to severe burnout, public-sector practitioners disproportionately affected (Eleos Health, 2024).

The core constraint in this case is data sensitivity. Under GDPR Article 9(1), psychological health data is classified as special category personal data requiring the highest level of protection (GDPR, 2018). Spain's LOPDGDD (Ley Orgánica 3/2018) reinforces this at national level, adding conditions for processing health data beyond the GDPR baseline. Any use of external AI tools such as ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, or Google Gemini would involve transmitting patient-related content to third-party servers outside the organization's control, a direct legal and ethical violation. The organization needs AI assistance, but the data must never leave its own infrastructure.

## Personas

**Persona 1: Clinical Psychologist.** Conducts 6 to 8 therapy sessions per day. After each session, she must write structured case notes, cross-reference previous session observations, and check whether the patient's evolving profile triggers any mandatory reporting protocols (e.g. risk of self-harm). Currently this takes 35–40 minutes of manual document review per day. Her core pain point is the lack of a searchable knowledge base: she knows the protocol exists somewhere but finding it in time between sessions is a constant source of stress.

**Persona 2: Social Worker.** Coordinates care between psychologists, external partner organizations (housing services, emergency shelters, legal aid), and family units. He drafts formal referral letters and inter-agency reports, often needing to produce 3 to 5 documents per week. Each document currently takes 90–120 minutes to produce from scratch, pulling from multiple sources. His pain point is the absence of any templated, context-aware drafting tool, every report starts from a blank page.

**Persona 3: Volunteer Coordinator.** Onboards new volunteers, manages scheduling, and fields recurring operational questions from a pool of 35 active volunteers. She answers the same 12 questions repeatedly (intake procedures, safeguarding escalation paths, risk assessment forms). Her pain point is that there is no self-service knowledge base, forcing her to interrupt clinical staff to answer questions that should be automated.

## Journey

**Step 1: Psychologist uploads session transcript.** After a session, María opens LobeChat at https://52-211-99-217.sslip.io, the self-hosted instance running on the organization's AWS EC2 (eu-west-1, Ireland inside the EEA). She uploads the session transcript as a PDF. The file is stored in MinIO (:47005), the self-hosted S3-compatible object storage running on the same EC2, encrypted at rest. No data leaves the organization's AWS environment.

**Step 2: RAG-powered protocol search.** María types: "Based on this session, does the patient meet the criteria for our mandatory risk-of-harm escalation protocol?" LobeChat forwards the query to MCPHub (:47008), which routes it to the haystack-mcp MCP server exposing Hayhooks (:47012). Hayhooks embeds the query and runs a semantic search against Qdrant (:47010), previously indexed with all of the organization's clinical protocols and treatment guidelines (~2,400 documents). The LLM (accessed via OpenRouter using only anonymised, de-identified query text, no patient names or case IDs) generates a structured response with direct references to the relevant protocol sections. Time saved: estimated 20 minutes per day versus manual search.

**Step 3: Social worker drafts a referral letter.** Javier asks LobeChat: "Draft a formal referral letter to the municipal housing service for a 32-year-old male patient with chronic depression and no fixed address." MCPHub routes to the filesystem MCP server, which retrieves the organization's referral template from MinIO (:47005). Hayhooks pulls relevant regulatory language from Qdrant. PostgreSQL (:47003) supplies case metadata. The LLM produces a complete draft in under 30 seconds. Javier reviews and edits, total time: 15 minutes instead of 90-120. Estimated weekly saving: 4 to 6 hours.

**Step 4: Volunteer self-service query.** A new volunteer asks Sara which intake form to use for a minor presenting with anxiety. Sara directs her to LobeChat. The volunteer types the question directly; MCPHub routes to haystack-mcp, which retrieves the correct form reference from Qdrant along with the relevant safeguarding checklist. No senior staff member needs to be interrupted. Sara estimates this saves her 45 minutes per week.

**Data flow summary:**

| Layer | Service | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| Storage | MinIO (:47005) | Encrypted at rest, private EC2 subnet |
| Vector search | Qdrant (:47010) | All indexing runs locally on EC2 |
| LLM queries | OpenRouter | Anonymised, de-identified text only, no patient names or case IDs |
| Tool invocations | MCPHub (:47008) | File retrieval, report formatting, MCP server routing |
| Authentication | Casdoor (:47002) | SSO via SAML 2.0, role-based access: psychologists see only their own cases |

## Why this stack vs ChatGPT/Copilot

**1. Legal compliance is non-negotiable.** GDPR Article 9 is the most stringent provision in the GDPR — it treats health data as inherently dangerous and requires both a lawful basis under Article 6 and an explicit Article 9 exception, with violations carrying fines of up to €20M or 4% of global turnover. Spain's LOPDGDD adds further national restrictions beyond the GDPR baseline. OpenAI's data processing agreements for ChatGPT Enterprise do not satisfy the requirements for special category data under AEPD guidelines — inference still runs on infrastructure outside the organization's direct control, which the AEPD's 2026 agentic AI orientations explicitly flag as a risk factor requiring mitigation. Self-hosting LobeChat on EC2 with MinIO ensures zero data egress. This is not a preference — it is a legal requirement.

**2. Proprietary knowledge base via RAG.** ChatGPT has no access to the organization's internal protocols, case templates, or referral directories unless manually pasted into every prompt. Qdrant + Hayhooks enables semantic indexing of the entire document library (~2,400 documents), allowing staff to query institutional knowledge naturally via MCPHub's tool routing. This is structurally impossible with a SaaS AI tool without building a custom integration at significant additional cost.

**3. Predictable cost at NGO scale.** ChatGPT Business is priced at $25 per user per month billed annually — for 80 users, that is $2,000/month or $24,000/year, a significant burden for a grant-funded NGO with strict overhead caps.This stack runs on a t3.xlarge EC2 instance at approximately $150/month plus OpenRouter API costs (estimated $40–80/month at current usage), totalling under $4,400/year. Annual saving: over $19,000, enough to fund a part-time clinical support role.

However, this stack is not without operational costs. Maintaining the EC2 instance, Docker updates, and Casdoor's SSO configuration requires technical capacity that a grant-funded NGO may not have in-house — a burden that a managed SaaS product eliminates entirely. LLM quality via OpenRouter's lower-tier models also trails GPT-4o in nuanced clinical reasoning, meaning the organization would likely need a paid plan ($50–100/month) to close that gap. These are real costs, but they are predictable and budgetable, unlike the legal exposure of routing Article 9 data through a third-party server.
